Chicago Home Renovation Permits: Everything You Need to Know

May 5, 2026 | Crestline Remodeling Team

If you are planning a home renovation in the Chicago area, understanding the permitting process is essential. Building permits exist to ensure that construction work meets safety standards, zoning requirements, and building codes. In this guide, we cover everything you need to know about home renovation permits in Chicago and the surrounding suburbs.

When Do You Need a Permit?

In general, you need a building permit for any renovation that involves structural changes, electrical work, plumbing work, or mechanical (HVAC) work. Here is a breakdown:

Projects That Typically Require a Permit

  • Kitchen remodels that involve moving or adding plumbing, electrical, or gas lines
  • Bathroom remodels that involve moving plumbing or adding new fixtures
  • Basement finishing (framing, electrical, plumbing, egress windows)
  • Room additions and home expansions
  • Removing or modifying walls (especially load-bearing walls)
  • Electrical panel upgrades or new circuit installation
  • New window or door openings
  • Deck and patio construction
  • Roofing replacement (some municipalities)
  • HVAC system replacement or relocation
  • Water heater replacement
  • Fence installation over a certain height

Projects That Usually Do Not Require a Permit

  • Painting and wallpaper
  • Replacing existing flooring (same subfloor)
  • Cabinet refacing or replacement (no plumbing or electrical changes)
  • Countertop replacement (no plumbing changes)
  • Replacing fixtures in the same location (faucet, light fixture, outlet cover)
  • Minor cosmetic updates

When in doubt, check with your local building department. It is always better to get a permit you do not technically need than to skip one you do.

The Permit Process in Chicago

In the City of Chicago, building permits are managed by the Department of Buildings (DOB). The process has been increasingly digitized in recent years through the city's E-Plan system.

Step 1: Determine Permit Type

Chicago offers several permit types for residential work. The most common for home renovations is the Standard Plan Review permit, which requires plan submission and review. Simpler projects may qualify for an Easy Permit, which can be issued faster with less documentation. Your contractor can advise on which type applies to your project.

Step 2: Prepare Documentation

Depending on the scope, you may need architectural drawings, structural engineering calculations, a plat of survey, a contractor's license information, and proof of insurance. For more complex projects (additions, structural modifications), a licensed architect's stamped plans are required.

Step 3: Submit Application

Applications are submitted through the city's online portal or in person at City Hall. The application includes project details, contractor information, property ownership verification, and the appropriate fees.

Step 4: Plan Review

The DOB reviews submitted plans for compliance with Chicago's building code, zoning ordinance, and fire code. Review times vary -- simple projects may be reviewed in 1-2 weeks, while complex projects can take 4-8 weeks. If corrections are needed, the plans are returned for revision and resubmission.

Step 5: Permit Issuance

Once plans are approved, the permit is issued. The permit card must be posted at the job site throughout construction. Work can begin once the permit is in hand.

Step 6: Inspections

During construction, the building department conducts inspections at key stages: rough-in (framing, plumbing, electrical), insulation, and final. All inspections must pass before the project can be officially closed out. Your contractor schedules these inspections and is present to address any inspector questions.

Suburban Permit Processes

Each suburban municipality in the Chicago area manages its own permitting process. While the general framework is similar to Chicago, there are important differences:

  • Review timelines: Some villages (Winnetka, Hinsdale, Lake Forest) have architectural review boards that add a layer of design review beyond code compliance. This can add 2-4 weeks to the approval process.
  • Contractor licensing: Many suburbs require contractors to hold a village-specific license or register with the building department before pulling permits. Crestline Remodeling maintains current registrations across the North Shore and western suburbs.
  • Historic district requirements: Communities like Oak Park, Lake Forest, and Highland Park have historic preservation ordinances that may impose additional requirements on renovations to homes in designated historic districts.
  • Zoning setbacks and FAR: Additions and exterior changes must comply with local zoning, including setback requirements, floor area ratio (FAR) limits, and impervious surface calculations. These regulations vary significantly by municipality.

Permit Costs

Permit fees in the Chicago area vary by municipality and project scope. Here are typical ranges:

  • City of Chicago: $75 for an Easy Permit, $250 to $2,000+ for Standard Plan Review permits, depending on project value.
  • Suburban municipalities: $100 to $5,000, typically calculated as a percentage of project value (often 1-2%) plus flat fees for plan review and inspections.
  • Architectural review fees: $100 to $500 in villages with design review boards.

Permit costs are a standard part of any remodeling project budget, and your contractor should include them in the project proposal.

What Happens If You Skip the Permit?

Renovating without required permits carries real risks:

  • Stop-work orders: If an inspector discovers unpermitted work in progress, they can issue a stop-work order, halting the project until permits are obtained. This causes delays and additional costs.
  • Fines: Municipalities can impose fines for unpermitted work, ranging from hundreds to thousands of dollars.
  • Insurance issues: If unpermitted work causes damage (fire, water leak, structural failure), your homeowner's insurance may deny the claim.
  • Sale complications: When you sell your home, buyers, their inspectors, and their lenders may discover unpermitted work. This can derail a sale or require you to retroactively permit and bring work up to code -- a costly and time-consuming process.
  • Safety risks: Permits and inspections exist to protect your family. Unpermitted electrical, plumbing, and structural work may not meet code, creating genuine safety hazards.
