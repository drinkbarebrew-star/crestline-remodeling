Best Countertop Materials for Chicago Kitchens: Complete Comparison Guide

May 20, 2026 | Crestline Remodeling Team

Choosing the right countertop material is one of the most important decisions in any kitchen remodel. Your countertops need to be beautiful, durable, and practical for your cooking and entertaining habits. In this guide, we compare the most popular countertop materials for Chicago kitchens, covering appearance, durability, maintenance, cost, and best use cases.

Quartz

Quartz has become the most popular countertop material in the Chicago market, and for good reason. Engineered from natural quartz crystals bound with resin, quartz countertops offer the beauty of natural stone with superior consistency and lower maintenance.

Appearance: Available in a vast range of colors and patterns, including designs that closely mimic marble, granite, and concrete. The engineered manufacturing process ensures consistent patterning, which some homeowners prefer over the unpredictability of natural stone.

Durability: Extremely durable and resistant to scratches, chips, and cracks under normal kitchen use. Quartz is non-porous, which means it will not stain or harbor bacteria without sealing.

Maintenance: Clean with soap and water. No sealing required, ever. Quartz is one of the lowest-maintenance countertop options available.

Cost: $60 to $120 per square foot installed in the Chicago area. Premium brands and patterns can reach $150+.

Considerations: Quartz is not recommended for outdoor use, as prolonged UV exposure can cause discoloration. It can also be damaged by extreme heat -- always use a trivet for hot pots and pans.

Granite

Granite was the gold standard for kitchen countertops for decades, and it remains a popular and practical choice in 2026. Each slab is unique, offering natural beauty that cannot be exactly replicated.

Appearance: Naturally occurring patterns with depth and movement. Available in hundreds of colors, from subtle whites and grays to dramatic blacks and blues. Each slab is one of a kind.

Durability: Very hard and scratch-resistant. Granite handles heat well -- you can place a hot pan directly on the surface without damage (though we recommend trivets as a habit).

Maintenance: Requires sealing once a year to maintain stain resistance. The sealing process is simple and takes about 15 minutes for an average kitchen.

Cost: $50 to $100 per square foot installed. Exotic granites with rare colors and dramatic veining can cost $100 to $200+.

Considerations: Some granite colors and patterns have become less fashionable in recent years, so choose a classic pattern if resale value is a priority. Granite is also heavier than quartz, which may require additional cabinet reinforcement.

Marble

Marble is the ultimate luxury countertop material, prized for its timeless elegance and natural veining. It is also one of the most high-maintenance options, which makes it a better fit for some kitchens than others.

Appearance: Unmatched natural beauty with distinctive veining. Calacatta and Carrara are the most popular varieties for Chicago kitchens. The luminous quality of polished marble is simply unrivaled.

Durability: Softer than granite and quartz, making it more susceptible to scratching and chipping. Marble is also porous and can etch from acidic foods (lemon, tomato, wine).

Maintenance: Requires regular sealing (every 3-6 months for heavy-use kitchens) and careful cleaning. Many homeowners embrace the patina that develops over time as part of marble's character.

Cost: $75 to $200+ per square foot installed. Bookmatched slabs (mirror-imaged pairs) and rare quarry selections can exceed $300.

Considerations: If you love the look of marble but want lower maintenance, consider a marble-look quartz. If you are drawn to authentic marble and understand the maintenance commitment, it delivers beauty that no engineered product can fully replicate.

Butcher Block

Butcher block countertops bring natural warmth and a lived-in character to the kitchen. They are particularly popular in farmhouse, transitional, and cottage-style kitchens.

Appearance: Warm, natural wood grain in species like maple, walnut, cherry, and white oak. Available in edge-grain, end-grain, and wide-plank configurations, each with a distinct visual character.

Durability: Softer than stone, butcher block will scratch and dent over time. However, this is often considered part of its charm. Minor scratches and stains can be sanded out and refinished.

Maintenance: Requires regular oiling (monthly for the first year, quarterly thereafter) with food-safe mineral oil or a beeswax blend. Should not be used as a direct cutting surface, despite the name.

Cost: $40 to $100 per square foot installed, depending on wood species and construction method.

Considerations: Not suitable for areas near the sink if you are not diligent about keeping water wiped up. Many designers recommend using butcher block on an island or secondary prep area, paired with a more water-resistant material around the sink.

Quartzite

Not to be confused with quartz (engineered stone), quartzite is a natural stone formed from sandstone under intense heat and pressure. It offers the beauty of marble with significantly greater hardness.

Appearance: Stunning natural veining similar to marble but with a more crystalline, luminous quality. Popular varieties include Taj Mahal, Fantasy Brown, and Super White.

Durability: Harder than granite and significantly harder than marble. Quartzite is highly resistant to scratching and etching from acidic foods.

Maintenance: Requires periodic sealing (annually) to maintain stain resistance. Cleaning with soap and water is sufficient for daily maintenance.

Cost: $80 to $200+ per square foot installed. Quartzite is at the premium end of natural stone pricing.

Considerations: Quartzite is an excellent choice for homeowners who love the look of marble but want better durability. Verify that your "quartzite" is genuinely quartzite, as some dealers mislabel soft dolomite or marble as quartzite.

Solid Surface

Solid surface materials like Corian and Wilsonart offer a seamless, integrated look at a moderate price point. They are a practical choice for bathrooms and secondary kitchen areas.

Appearance: Uniform color and pattern. Available in hundreds of colors. Seams can be virtually invisible, creating a continuous surface. Integrated sinks and backsplashes are possible.

Durability: Softer than stone and susceptible to scratching, but minor damage can be sanded and buffed out. Moderate heat resistance -- always use trivets.

Maintenance: Non-porous and easy to clean. No sealing required. Minor scratches can be repaired with a Scotch-Brite pad.

Cost: $40 to $80 per square foot installed.

Considerations: Solid surface has fallen somewhat out of fashion for primary kitchen countertops in favor of quartz and natural stone. However, it remains an excellent value for laundry rooms, bathrooms, and secondary workspaces.

Making Your Decision

The best countertop material for your kitchen depends on your priorities. If you want the lowest maintenance, choose quartz. If you want natural beauty with proven durability, consider granite or quartzite. If you are drawn to timeless luxury and do not mind the upkeep, marble is unmatched. And if warmth and character are your top priorities, butcher block adds something no stone can.
